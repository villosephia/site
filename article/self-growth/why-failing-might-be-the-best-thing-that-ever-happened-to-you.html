Why Failing Might Be the Best Thing That Ever Happened to You?

Self Growth - 15/01/2025

Let’s be real failing sucks. You pour your heart into something, hoping it’ll work out, and then… boom, it crashes and burns. You start spiraling “Am I even good enough? Should I just quit? What’s the point?” If you’ve been there, trust me, same. And honestly? That failure turned out to be the best thing that ever happened to me.

Here’s the story. A few years ago, I had this big dream of starting a creative community for young people in my city. I wanted it to be this cool, safe space where we could exchange ideas, learn from each other, and grow together. I was so hyped about it. I planned everything down to the tiniest detail events, venues, social media promos. I was 100% sure it was gonna be amazing.

Then, the day of the event came. And guess what? Out of the dozens of people who signed up online, only two showed up. TWO. Imagine standing there in this huge, empty room, smiling and pretending everything was fine while your confidence crumbles inside. I was devastated. I went home that night feeling like a total failure.

For days, I couldn’t stop replaying it in my head. “What went wrong? Was it me? Should I just give up?” It was a dark place to be in. But then, after letting myself feel all the feels, I decided to look at it differently. Instead of focusing on what went wrong, I asked myself, “What can I learn from this?”

Turns out, failure is the ultimate teacher. I realized I was so caught up in the big picture that I didn’t think enough about what people actually wanted. My marketing sucked, and honestly, my execution could’ve been way better. It wasn’t about me being “not good enough.” It was about me needing to improve.

So, I tried again. A year later, I relaunched with a smaller, more focused event. This time, I made sure to actually connect with people first. I listened to what they wanted and tailored everything to fit their needs. Slowly but surely, the community started growing. People came back, not just because of the events, but because they felt seen and valued.

Looking back, failing was exactly what I needed. If that first event had been “perfect,” I wouldn’t have learned half the lessons I did. Failure pushed me to be better, to work smarter, and to grow in ways I never imagined.

So, why might failing be the best thing that ever happens to you?

Because it forces you to pause, reflect, and get real with yourself. It teaches you resilience and helps you level up in ways success never could. Failures aren’t the end of the story they’re just the messy middle chapters that make the whole book worth reading.
